Section 2 Three examples

A fundamental result due to Rudin and Stout (see \cite[Chapter 5]{Rud69}) asserts that any RIF on \(\mathbb{D}^2\) can be written as

\begin{equation} \phi(z)=e^{i\alpha}z_1^{\beta_1}z_ 2^{\beta_2}\frac{\tilde{p}(z)}{p(z)},\label{eq-rudin}\tag{2.1} \end{equation}

where \(\alpha\in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\beta_1,\beta_2 \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the \(p\in\mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) has no zeros in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{,}\) and \(\tilde{p}\) is the reflection of \(p\text{,}\) defined as

\begin{equation} \tilde{p}(z_1,z_2)=z_1^mz_2^n\overline{p\left(\frac{1}{\bar{z}_1},\frac{1}{\bar{z}_2}\right)}.\label{eq-preflection}\tag{2.2} \end{equation}

Here the pair \((m,n)\) is the bidegree of \(p\text{,}\) that is \(p\) has degree \(m\) in \(z_1\) and degree \(n\) in \(z_2\text{.}\)

This structural result makes examples of rational inner functions particularly easy to construct - all one requires is a polynomial with no zeros in the bidisk. The following such examples can be fruitfully analyzed using elementary means.

Example 2.1.

Consider the RISP

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z_1,z_2)=\left(-\frac{2z_1z_2-z_1-z_2}{2-z_1-z_2},z_2\right). \end{equation*}

The first component \(\phi=-\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\) is a degree \((1,1)\) RIF having a single singularity at \((1,1)\text{.}\) A computation reveals that the boundary value \(\phi^{*}(1,1)=1\text{,}\) and so \((1,1)\) is a fixed point of \(\Phi\text{.}\) In fact, we have \(\Phi(z_1,1)=(1,1)\text{,}\) so the entire line \(\{z_2=1\}\) is mapped to \((1,1)\text{.}\) Next, we note that \(\phi^*(1,z_2)=1\) and \(\phi^*(z_2,z_2)=z_2\) meaning that both the line \(\{z_1=1\}\) and the diagonal are comprised of fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\) Solving \(\phi^{*}(z)=z_1\text{,}\) or more precisely, the equation

\begin{equation*} \tilde{p}(z)-z_1p(z)=0, \end{equation*}

confirms that these are all the fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

We now iterate \(\Phi\text{.}\) We visualize the action of the iterates by viewing the \(2\)-torus as \([-\pi, \pi]^2\) and applying \(\Phi^n\) to vertical lines of the form \(\{(a\pi, t_2)\}\) for several choices of \(-1\lt a\lt 1\text{.}\) FigureÂ 2.2 suggests that the iterates converge as \(n\) grows. Using induction, one can show that, for \(n=1,2,\ldots\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \Phi^n(z_1,z_2)=\left(-\frac{2^nz_1z_2-z_1-(2^{n}-1)z_2}{2^n-(2^{n}-1)z_1-z_2}, z_2\right). \end{equation*}

Hence, for all \((z_1,z_2)\in \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\setminus \{z_1=1\}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \Phi^n(z_1,z_2)\to (z_2,z_2) \quad \textrm{as} \quad n\to \infty. \end{equation*}

This means that each point on the diagonal is attractive on its corresponding horizontal fiber, while points on \(\{z_1=1\}\) are repelling fixed points. The special fiber \(\{z_2=1\}\) is immediately collapsed into \((1,1)\) by \(\Phi\text{,}\) explaining the pinched appearance of the images.

The special role the lines \(\{z_1=1\}\) and \(\{z_2=1\}\) play here is related to the fact that they are the level sets of \(\phi\) corresponding to the non-tangential value \(\lambda=1\text{,}\) which is attained at the singularity of \(\phi\) at \((1,1)\) (see [8]). This will be discussed in detail below.
