Section 3 Preliminaries

Subsection 3.1 Rational inner functions on the bidisk

Recall that a RIF is of the form

\begin{equation*} \phi(z)=e^{i\alpha}z_1^{\beta_1}z_ 2^{\beta_2}\frac{\tilde{p}(z)}{p(z)}. \end{equation*}

In what follows, we shall frequently assume that our RIFs are normalized. We take this to mean \(\beta_1=\beta_2=0\text{,}\) meaning that

\begin{equation*} \phi=e^{i\alpha}\frac{\tilde{p}}{p} \end{equation*}

for some polynomial \(p\) with no zeros in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) We also assume that \(p\) is atoral in the sense of [2], meaning that \(p\) and \(\tilde{p}\) have no common factors. (If \(p\) is not initially atoral, then any toral factors can be canceled with the corresponding toral factors in \(\tilde{p}\text{.}\))

Let us review some basic facts concerning rational inner functions on the bidisk. By the definition of the reflection operation, any \(\tau \in\mathbb{T}^2\) that is a zero of \(p\) is a zero of \(\tilde{p}\text{.}\) We say that \(\tau\in\mathbb{T}^2\) is a singular point of \(\phi\) if \(p(\tau)=0\) (and hence also \(\tilde{p}(\tau)=0\)). Next, let us recall Bézout's theorem for \(\mathbb{C}_{\infty}\times \mathbb{C}_{\infty}\text{,}\) as discussed in [18](Section 12). Namely, if \(P, Q\in \mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) are polynomials with no common factors and with \(\operatorname{deg}P=(M_1,N_1)\) and \(\operatorname{deg} Q=(M_2,N_2)\text{,}\) then \(P\) and \(Q\) have \(M_1N_2+M_2N_1\) common zeros in \(\mathbb{C}_{\infty}\times \mathbb{C}_{\infty}\text{.}\) Applying this result to \(P=p\) and \(Q=\tilde{p}\) shows that any two-variable rational inner function has at most finitely many singularities on \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\)

Since \(\phi\) is a bounded analytic function, Fatou's theorem for polydisks guarantees the existence of non-tangential limits of \(\phi\text{:}\) that is,

\begin{equation*} \phi^*(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)=\angle\lim_{\mathbb{D}^2\ni (z_1,z_2)\to (\zeta_1,\zeta_2)}\phi(z_1,z_2) \end{equation*}

exists at almost every \(\zeta \in \mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) Here, \(\angle\lim_{z\to \zeta}f(z)\) denotes letting \(z\) tend to \(\zeta \in \mathbb{T}^2\) with \(|z_j-\zeta_j|\lt c(1-|z_j|)\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{,}\) for some constant \(c>1\text{.}\) Knese proved [18](Corollary 14.6) that if \(\phi\) is rational inner function, then the non-tangential limit \(\phi^*(\zeta)\) exists and is unimodular at every point \(\zeta \in \T^2\text{.}\) However, if a normalized \(\phi\) has singular points, then \(\phi^*(\zeta)\) is not typically continuous on \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) See [3, 27, 8, 9] for more background material on RIFs.

Definition 3.1.

Let \(\Phi=(\phi_1, \phi_2)\colon \mathbb{D}^2\to \mathbb{D}^2\) be a non-constant normalized RIM. We say that \(\tau\in \mathbb{T}^2\) is a singular fixed point} (SF-point) of \(\Phi\) if

  • \(\tau \in\mathbb{T}^2\) is a singular point of at least one of \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\text{;}\)
  • \(\tau\) is a fixed point: \(\Phi^*(\tau)=\tau\text{.}\)

Note that, for each denominator \(p\text{,}\) we can make sure the corresponding component RIFs \(\phi_j\) satisfy the condition \(\phi^*_j(\tau)=\tau_j\) by a suitable choice of unimodular factor \(e^{i\alpha_j}\text{.}\) If \(\phi_j\) possesses multiple singularities then we cannot typically normalize all of them to be SF-points simultaneously.

We record a few further facts about rational inner functions. Suppose \(\phi\) is RIF of bidegree \((m,n)\text{.}\) For a fixed \(\zeta_2\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the function

\begin{equation*} \phi_{\zeta_2}\colon z_1\mapsto \phi(z_1,\zeta_2) \end{equation*}

is a bounded rational function in the unit disk, attaining unimodular boundary values at every point of \(\mathbb{T}\text{.}\) Hence \(\phi_{\zeta_2}\) is constant, a monomial, or a finite Blaschke product of degree \(1\leq d\leq n\text{.}\) Returning to the setting of a RISP \(\Phi=(\phi, z_2)\text{,}\) we note that if \(\lambda\) is a constant, \(\Phi\) maps the set

\begin{equation} F_{\lambda}=\{(z_1,z_2)\in \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\colon z_2=\lambda\}\label{fiber}\tag{3.1} \end{equation}

into itself. The set \(F_{\lambda}\) is referred to as a fiber. On each \(F_{\lambda}\text{,}\) the first component \(\phi_{\lambda}\) is generically a finite Blaschke product of degree \(n\) but on certain fibers the degree may drop. The most extreme examples are the following.

Definition 3.2.

Let \(\Phi=(\phi,z_2)\) be a normalized RISP, and let \(\lambda \in\mathbb{T}\text{.}\) We say that \(F_{\lambda}\) is a collapsing fiber for \(\Phi\) if the one-variable function \(\phi_{\lambda}\) is constant.

We often specialize to bidegree \((1,n)\) RIFs. In that case, we write \(\phi=e^{i\alpha}\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation} p(z)=p_1(z_2)+z_1p_2(z_2), \label{def-p1p2polys}\tag{3.2} \end{equation}

for some \(p_1,p_2\in \mathbb{C}[z_2]\text{.}\) Then as a consequence \(\tilde{p}(z)=\tilde{p}_2(z_2)+z_1\tilde{p}_1(z_2)\text{,}\) where each \(\tilde{p}_j(z_2)=z_2^n\overline{p}(1/\bar{z}_2)\text{.}\) Note that \(p_1(z_2)\neq 0\) for \(z_2 \in \mathbb {D}\) since \(p\) is assumed to have no zeros in the bidisk, and in fact \(p_1(z_2)\neq 0\) for \(z\in \overline{\mathbb{D}}\) by [18][Lemma 10.1].

Finally, for \(\alpha\in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) let us define the polynomial

\begin{equation} Q_{\alpha}(z_2)=(p_1(z_2)-e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(z_2))^2+4e^{i\alpha}p_2(z_2)\tilde{p}_2(z_2)\label{def-qapoly}\tag{3.3} \end{equation}

which we shall later use to analyze the fixed points of the corresponding RISP \((\phi, z_2)\text{.}\) Note that if \(\phi\) has bidegree \((1,n)\text{,}\) then \(Q_{\alpha}\) has degree at most \(2n\text{,}\) and that \(\tilde{Q}_{\alpha}(z_2)=e^{-2i\alpha}Q_{\alpha}(z_2)\text{.}\)

When \(\phi\) is a degree \((1,n)\) RIF and \(\zeta_2\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the one-variable function \(\phi_{\zeta_2}\) has \(\deg(\phi_{\zeta_2})=1\text{,}\) or is a constant and thus has a collapsing fiber \(F_{\zeta_2}\text{.}\) In this case, we can characterize collapsing fibers using a result from [6]: reversing the roles of \(z_1\) and \(z_2\text{,}\) [6][Theorem 3.3] yields the following.

Lemma 3.3.

Suppose \(\Phi=(\phi,z_2)\) is a normalized bidegree \((1,n)\) RISP. Then \(F_{\lambda}\) is a collapsing fiber if and only if \((\tau_1, \lambda)\in \mathbb{T}^2\) is a singularity of \(\phi\) for some choice of \(\tau_1\text{.}\) If this is the case, then \(\phi^*(\tau_1, \lambda)=\tau_1\) and \((\tau_1, \lambda)\) is an SF-point of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

In other words, Lemma 3.3 relates collapsing fibers of a RISP to the level sets of its first component map \(\phi\text{.}\) In this regard, two-variable rational inner functions exhibit better behavior than rational inner functions in \(\mathbb{D}^n\) when \(n\geq 3\text{.}\) In [9][Theorem 2.8], it is shown that for each fixed \(\lambda\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the unimodular level set

\begin{equation} \mathcal{C}_{\lambda}=\{(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)\in \mathbb{T}^2\colon \tilde{p}(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)-\lambda p(\zeta_1,\zeta_2)=0\}\label{eq-levelcurve}\tag{3.4} \end{equation}

consists of components that can be parametrized using analytic functions. By contrast, unimodular level sets in higher dimensions need not even be continuous [10].

Subsection 3.2 Dynamics of Möbius transformations

Recall that the elements of the conformal automorphism group \(\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{D})\) of the unit disk are Möbius transformations of the form

\begin{equation*} \mathfrak{m}(z)=e^{i\alpha}\frac{a-z}{1-\overline{a}z}, \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha \in \mathbb{T}\) and \(a\in \mathbb{D}\text{.}\) Note that each Möbius transformation extends to the closed bidisk \(\overline{\mathbb{D}}\) and in particular furnishes a smooth diffeomorphism of the unit circle \(\mathbb{T}\text{.}\)

If \(\phi\) is a bidegree \((1,n)\) rational inner function, then for each fixed \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the analytic function \(z_1\mapsto \phi(z_1,\lambda)\) is bounded, satisfies \(|\phi(z_1,\lambda)|=1\text{,}\) and is of degree \(0\) or \(1\text{.}\) Then either \(\phi(\cdot, \lambda)\) is a Möbius transformation for some values of \(\alpha(\lambda)\) and \(a(\lambda)\text{,}\) or \(\phi(\cdot, \lambda)\) is constant. The latter possibility corresponds to a collapsing fiber, and by Lemma 3.3, this occurs if only if \((\zeta_1,\lambda)\) is a singularity of \(\phi\) for some choice of \(\zeta_1\text{.}\)

In summary, for all but finitely many values

\begin{equation*} \Lambda^{\flat}=\{\lambda^{\flat}_1,\ldots, \lambda^{\flat}_N\} \subset \mathbb{T}, \end{equation*}

whose number is bounded by the degree of \(\phi\) in \(z_2\text{,}\) the fiber map

\begin{equation*} z_1\mapsto \phi_{\lambda}(z_1)=\phi(z_1,\lambda) \end{equation*}

is a Möbius transformation with parameters \(\alpha(\lambda)\) and \(a(\lambda)\text{.}\) The functions \(\alpha(\lambda)\) and \(a(\lambda)\) are continuous in any open set that does not intersect \(\Lambda^{\flat}\text{.}\) Associated with \(\Lambda^{\flat}\) is the set

\begin{equation} \iota(\Lambda^{\flat})=\bigcup_{\lambda \in \Lambda^{\flat}}F_{\lambda};\label{badfibers}\tag{3.5} \end{equation}

note that this is a finite union of horizontal lines in \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\)

Let us recall the well-known classification of Möbius transformations depending on their fixed points. See [5][Chapter 1] or [19][Chapter 1] for a background discussion.

Definition 3.4.

Suppose \(\mathfrak{m}\in \mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{D})\setminus\{\mathrm{id}\}\text{.}\) Then one of the following holds:

  • \(\mathfrak{m}\) is elliptic: \(\mathfrak{m}\) has one fixed point in \(\mathbb{D}\text{,}\) and no fixed points on \(\mathbb{T}\)
  • \(\mathfrak{m}\) is parabolic: \(\mathfrak{m}\) has no fixed points in \(\mathbb{D}\) and one fixed point on \(\mathbb{T}\)
  • \(\mathfrak{m}\) is hyperbolic: \(\mathfrak{m}\) has no fixed points in \(\mathbb{D}\) and two fixed points on \(\mathbb{T}\text{.}\)

Fixed points are classified according to the following scheme.

Definition 3.5.

Suppose \(t \in \overline{\mathbb{D}}\) is a fixed point of a Möbius transformation \(\mathfrak{m}\) and define the multiplier \(\mathfrak{p}=\mathfrak{m}'(t) \text{.}\) We say that

  • \(t\) is attracting if \(0\lt |\mathfrak{p}|\lt 1\)
  • \(t\) is repelling if \(|\mathfrak{p}|>1\)
  • \(t\) is indifferent if \(|\mathfrak{p}|=1\text{.}\)

We further distinguish between rationally indifferent fixed points arising when \(\mathfrak{p}\) is a root of unity, and irrationally indifferent fixed points corresponding to \(\mathfrak{p}\) unimodular but not a root of unity.

It is easy to give examples of Möbius transformations with these properties. The rotation \(\mathfrak{m}(z)=e^{i\alpha}z\) has a rationally or irrationally indifferent fixed point at \(t=0\) according to whether \(\alpha\) is rational or irrational. The map \(\mathfrak{m}(z)=\frac{(2+i)z+i}{2-i-iz}\) has a single fixed point at \(t=-1\text{,}\) and is parabolic. Finally, the hyperbolic map \(\mathfrak{m}(z)=\frac{7z_1-1}{7-z_1}\) has one attracting fixed point at \(t_1=-1\) and one attracting fixed point at \(t_2=1\text{.}\)

The dynamics of a Möbius transformation can be described depending on its type in the above list. Recall that two analytic functions \(f,g\colon \mathbb{D}\to \mathbb{D}\) are conformally conjugate if there exists a conformal map \(\mathfrak{n}\colon \mathbb{D}\to \mathbb{D}\) such that

\begin{equation*} g=\mathfrak{n}\circ f\circ \mathfrak{n}^{-1}. \end{equation*}

See [5][Chapter 2] or [19][Chapters 8-9] for general background. Note that conjugation preserves the multiplier of a Möbius transformation at a fixed point, leading to the following classification.

Proposition 3.6.

Let \(\mathfrak{m} \in \mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{D})\setminus\{\mathrm{id}\}\text{.}\) Then the following holds:

  • If \(\mathfrak{m}\) is elliptic, then \(\mathfrak{m}\) is conjugate to a rotation \(z\mapsto e^{ia}z\) with angle \(a=-i\log \mathfrak{p}\text{,}\) where \(\mathfrak{p}\) is the multiplier at the unique fixed point \(t\in \mathbb{D}\text{.}\)
  • If \(\mathfrak{m}\) is parabolic then \(\mathfrak{m}\) has multiplier equal to \(1\) at its unique fixed point \(t\in \mathbb{T}\text{.}\)
  • If \(\mathfrak{m}\) hyperbolic then \(\mathfrak{m}\) has one attracting fixed point \(t_1\in \mathbb{T}\) with associated multiplier \(\mathfrak{p}\in \mathbb{D}\) and one repelling fixed point \(t_2\in \mathbb{T}\) with multiplier \(\frac{1}{\mathfrak{p}}\text{.}\)
Proof.

See, for instance, [5][Chapter 1] or [19][Chapter 1].

Proposition 3.6 gives us a complete description of the possible dynamics of a bidegree \((1,n)\) RISPs on each fiber \(F_{\lambda}\text{.}\) In the next sections, we investigate how these dynamical properties vary along fibers.
