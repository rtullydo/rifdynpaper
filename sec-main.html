Section 4 RISP dynamics on the boundary

Subsection 4.1 General remarks on boundary fixed points

Consider a bidegree \((n_1,n_2)\) rational inner mapping \(\Phi=(\phi_1, \phi_2)\text{,}\) where \(\phi_j=e^{i\alpha_j}\frac{\tilde{p}_j}{p_j}\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{,}\) and where as usual each \(p_j \in \mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) has no zeros in \(\mathbb{D}\text{.}\) The set of fixed points of \(\Phi\) in \(\cc{\mathbb{D}^2}\) consists of all \(z \in \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\) such that

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z_1,z_2)=(z_1,z_2); \end{equation*}

recall that \(\Phi\) is well-defined at each \(\eta \in \mathbb{T}^2\) [18][Corollary 14.6].

We first record some facts about fixed points in the general case. For \(p_j\) and \(\tilde{p}_j\) as above, we define the auxiliary polynomials

\begin{equation} P_j(z)=e^{i\alpha_j}\tilde{p}_j(z)-z_1p_j(z), \quad j=1,2,\label{eq-fppolydef}\tag{4.1} \end{equation}

and we set \(\Gamma(\Phi)=\mathcal{Z}(P_1)\cap \mathcal{Z}(P_2)\cap \cc{\mathbb{D}^2}\text{.}\) Then \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) is comprised of the fixed points of \(\Phi\) along with all singular points of \(\Phi\text{.}\) As before, we adopt the convention that at least one of these is made into an SF-point by renormalizing the component maps \(\phi_j\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{.}\) Note that

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{deg} P_1=(n_{1}+1, n_{2}) \quad \textrm{and} \quad \operatorname{deg} P_2=(n_1,n_2+1). \end{equation*}
The polynomials \(P_j\) belong to a special class of polynomials that is often singled out in function theory in polydisks. We follow [2] and use the following definition.
Definition 4.1.
We say that \(P\in \mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) is essentially \(\mathbb{T}^2\)-symmetric if \(\tilde{P}=e^{i\beta}P\) for some \(\beta \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
We then have the following.
Lemma 4.2.

If \(\Phi\) is a normalized RIM, then the associated polynomials \(P_1,P_2\) defined in (4.1) are essentially \(\mathbb{T}^2\)-symmetric.

Proof.

This amounts to a computation. Applying the reflection operation (2.2) to the polynomial \(P_1\) and exploiting the fact that reflection is an involution, we obtain

\begin{gather*} \tilde{P}_1(z_1,z_2)=z_1^{n_1+1}z_2^{n_2}e^{-i\alpha_1}\cc{\tilde{p}}_1\left(\frac{1}{\cc{z}_1}, \frac{1}{\cc{z}_2}\right)-z_1^{n_1+1}z_2^{n_2}\frac{1}{z_1}\cc{p}_1\left(\frac{1}{\cc{z}_1}, \frac{1}{\cc{z}_2}\right)\\ =e^{-i\alpha_1}z_1p_1(z_1,z_2)-\tilde{p}_1(z_1,z_2)=-e^{-i\alpha_1}(-z_1p_1(z_1,z_2)+e^{i\alpha_1}\tilde{p}_1(z_1,z_2))\\=-e^{-i\alpha_1}P_1. \end{gather*}

A similar computation establishes the assertion for \(P_2\text{.}\)

Essentially \(\mathbb{T}^2\)-symmetric polynomials have been studied extensively, see for instance [2, 17, 9] and the references therein. In particular, such polynomials are related to determinantal representations and so-called distinguished varieties. We caution that the focus is often on essentially \(\mathbb{T}^2\)-symmetric polynomials with no zeros in the bidisk, while the polynomials \(P_j\) that we encounter in this work typically do have some zeros in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{,}\) namely at interior fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\) As a consequence, some of the known results from the literature (e.g. [7]) no longer apply.

In the pictures in Section 2, we observed the presence of curves of fixed points in the \(2\)-torus. The fixed point set of a general RIM, however, typically consists of isolated points. This can be seen via a standard application of Bézout's theorem. (See [25][Section 1.3] for applications to counting the number of fixed and periodic points in the context of iteration of rational functions in \(\mathbb{P}^k\text{.}\))

Lemma 4.3.

Suppose \(\Phi=(\phi_1, \phi_2)\) is a RIM such that the associated polynomials \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) have no common factors. Then \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) is a finite set, with cardinality bounded by \(2n_1n_2+n_1+n_2\text{.}\)

Proof.
Lemma 4.3 follows directly from Bézout's theorem as discussed in Section 3. Namely, since \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) have no common zeros, we have
\begin{equation*} \#(\mathcal{Z}(P_1)\cap \mathcal{Z}(P_2))=(n_1+1)\cdot(n_2+1) +n_2\cdot n_1=2n_1n_2+n_1+n_2. \end{equation*}
This is an upper bound on the number of elements of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) since some common zeros could be located in \((\overline{\mathbb{D}^2})^c\text{.}\)

Note that the assumption that \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) have no common factor is important: if we set \(\phi_1\) and \(\phi_2\) both equal to the RIF in Example 2.1, then \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) are both divisible by \(z_1-z_2\text{,}\) and the diagonal \(\{z_1=z_2\}\) is fixed by the resulting mapping \(\Phi\text{.}\)

We now focus on fixed points of a RISP \(\Phi=(\phi,z_2)\text{,}\) where \(\phi=\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\text{,}\) and \(p\) has no zeros in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) Note that for such functions, the polynomial \(P_2\) in (4.1) becomes identically 0, and so Lemma 4.3 does not apply. Instead, in this case, we get that \(z \in \mathbb{T}^2\) satisfies the single condition

\begin{equation} q(z)-z_1p(z)=0,\label{eq-singlefix}\tag{4.2} \end{equation}

and we can expect to see curves of fixed points in \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) Our goal is to investigate the nature of these fixed point curves.

We begin with a simple consequence of work in [9].
Proposition 4.4.

Let \(\Phi=(z_1\phi, z_2)\text{,}\) where \(\phi\) is a normalized RIF. Then \(\Gamma(\Phi)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) is a union of curves, and each component of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) can be parametrized by analytic functions.

The subtlety here is that \(\Phi\) may have singularities on \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{,}\) so that the implicit function theorem does not apply directly.

Proof.

By assumption, the first coordinate of \(\Phi\) can be written

\begin{equation*} z_1 \phi = z_1e^{i\alpha_1}\frac{\tilde{p}(z_1,z_2)}{p(z_1,z_2)} \end{equation*}

for some real \(\alpha_1\) and some polynomial \(p\) with no zeros on \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) Then, the fixed point condition (4.2) takes on the form

\begin{equation*} 0=\eta_1e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}(\eta)-\eta_1p(\eta)=\eta_1(e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}(\eta)-p(\eta)). \end{equation*}

Since \(\eta_1\neq 0\) for \(\eta_1\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) we get that \(\eta\in \mathbb{T}^2\) is a fixed point of \(\Phi\) if and only if \(e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}(\eta)-p(\eta)=0\text{.}\) This latter condition means that \(\eta\) belongs to some unimodular level set of the RIF \(\phi\text{.}\) Appealing to [9][Theorem 2.8, we arrive at the desired conclusion.

In particular, Proposition 4.4 implies that RISPs of the particular form \((z_1\phi, z_2)\) exhibit an abundance of fixed points in the \(2\)-torus. On each fiber, the function \(z_1\mapsto z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\) is a Blaschke product of degree at least \(1\text{,}\) and for all but finitely many values of \(\lambda\text{,}\) the degree is at least \(2\text{.}\) This means that the dynamical behavior on a \(\lambda\)-fiber generically does not admit a description in elementary terms.

For instance, \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\) has a fixed point at \(z_1=0\) for each choice of \(\lambda\) and this fixed point is attracting whenever \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}\) has degree at least \(2\text{.}\) Moreover since for generic \(\lambda\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the numerator of \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\) has higher degree than the denominator, the point at infinity of \(\mathbb{C}_{\infty}\) is also an attracting fixed point. This implies [19][Chapter 7] that for generic \(\lambda\text{,}\) the Julia set of the rational map \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\colon \mathbb{C}_{\infty}\to \mathbb{C}_{\infty}\) is \(\mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the unit circle. We thus have complicated dynamical behavior on each fiber \(F_{\lambda}\) and (see [5][Chapter 6] or [19][Chapter 4]), and for all but finitely many values of \(\lambda\in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) the points in \(F_{\lambda}\cap \Gamma(\phi)\) are repelling fixed points of the rational map \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\text{.}\) What Proposition 4.4 asserts is that these repelling fixed points move in an analytic fashion in the fiber variable \(\lambda\text{.}\)

The following example illustrates some of the facts listed above.

Example 4.5.

Consider the RISP

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z)=\left(-z_1\frac{2z_1z_2-z_1-z_2}{2-z_1-z_2}, z_2\right). \end{equation*}

As was noted above, the set \(\{(0,z_2)\}\subset \cc{\mathbb{D}^2}\) consists of fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\) On the fiber \(z_2=1\text{,}\) \(\Phi\) reduces to the identity, and on all other \(F_{\lambda}\text{,}\) the fiber map \(z_1\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)\) has degree \(2\) and has attracting fixed points at \(0\) and \(\infty\text{.}\)

Solving \(-(2z_1z_2-z_1-z_2)=2-z_1-z_2\text{,}\) we find that \(\{(1, \lambda)\colon \lambda \in \mathbb{T}\setminus \{1\}\}\subset \mathbb{T}^2\) is fixed by \(\Phi\text{,}\) so that \(1\in \mathbb{T}\) is a fixed point on each \(F_{\lambda}\text{.}\) A direct computation shows that \(\frac{d}{dz_1}(-z_1\phi_{\lambda})(1)=3\) when \(\lambda\neq 1\text{,}\) confirming the presence of a repelling fixed point.

The dynamics of \(\Phi\) are visualized in Figure 4.6 and indeed have a more complicated appearance than the examples in Section 2.

Subsection 4.2 Rotation belts and parabolic fixed points for simple RISPs

We return our focus to the simple case where \(\Phi=(\phi, z_2)\) and \(\phi\) is a bidegree \((1,n)\) normalized RIF, and where the fiber dynamics are completely described by Proposition 3.6. In this case, since \(p\) and \(\tilde{p}\) are polynomials of degree \(1\) in the first variable, (4.2) reduces to the quadratic equation

\begin{equation*} P(z_1,z_2)=e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}(z_1,z_2)-z_1p(z_1,z_2)=0. \end{equation*}

If \(\Phi\) has a SF-point in \(\mathbb{T}^2\) then \(\mathcal{Z}(P)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\neq \emptyset\) but it may well happen that \(\mathcal{Z}(P)\cap (F_{\lambda}\cap \mathbb{T}^2)=\emptyset\) for some values of \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T}\text{.}\) With this in mind, we make the following definition.

Definition 4.7.

Let \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\in [-\pi, \pi]\) and suppose \(\lambda_1\lt \lambda_2\text{.}\) We say that the set

\begin{equation*} B(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)=\mathbb{T}\times \{e^{it}\colon \lambda_1\lt t \lt \lambda_2 \}\subset \mathbb{T}^2 \end{equation*}

is a rotation belt for \(\Phi=(\phi, z_2)\) if

  • no point belonging to \(B(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)\) is a fixed point of \(\Phi\)
  • each of the sets \(F_{\lambda_1}\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) and \(F_{\lambda_2}\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) contains at least one fixed point of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

The reason for our terminology here is that by Proposition 3.6, each fiber map \(\phi_{\lambda}\) associated with fibers contained in \(B(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)\) is conjugate to a rotation. The second assumption is essentially a maximality condition. Example 2.1, Example 2.3 illustrate that not all RISPs possess rotation belts. Example 2.5 has a single rotation belt, but in general several rotation belts may be present.

We now give a criterion for a degree \((1,n)\) RISP to possess parabolic fixed points in terms of the one-variable polynomial \(Q_{\alpha}\text{.}\) As in (3.2) in Section 3, we write \(\phi=e^{i\alpha}\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} p(z)=p_1(z_2)+z_1p_2(z_2), \end{equation*}

and \(\tilde{p}(z)=\tilde{p}_2(z_2)+z_1\tilde{p}_1(z_2)\) for a pair of one-variable polynomials \(p_1,p_2\text{.}\) Recall that

\begin{equation*} Q_{\alpha}=(p_1-e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1)^2+4e^{i\alpha}p_2\tilde{p}_2. \end{equation*}

We shall need the auxiliary expressions

\begin{equation} \psi^{1}_{\alpha}(z_2)=\frac{1}{2p_2(z_2)}\left(p_1(z_2)-e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(z_2)+\sqrt{Q_{\alpha}(z_2)}\right)\label{psi1}\tag{4.3} \end{equation}

and

\begin{equation} \psi^{2}_{\alpha}(z_2)=\frac{1}{2p_2(z_2)}\left(p_1(z_2)-e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(z_2)-\sqrt{Q_{\alpha}(z_2)}\right).\label{psi2}\tag{4.4} \end{equation}

The functions \(\psi^j_{\alpha}\) parametrize the roots of the polynomial

\begin{equation*} P_ {\alpha}(z)=e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}-z_1p=e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_2+e^{i\alpha}z_1\tilde{p}_1-z_1p_1-z_1^2p_2 \end{equation*}

away from the set \(\Lambda^{\sharp}=\{z_2\in \mathbb{C}\colon p_2(z_2)=0\}\text{.}\) Note that \(\Lambda^{\flat}\cap \Lambda^{\sharp}=\emptyset\) since \(p_1(z_2)\neq 0\) for \(z_2\in \overline{\mathbb{D}}\text{.}\)

First, let us examine what happens on the set \(\Lambda^{\sharp}\text{.}\)

Lemma 4.8.

Suppose \(\lambda \in \mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha})\cap (\Lambda^{\sharp}\cap\mathbb{T})\text{.}\) Then \(\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)=e^{-i\alpha}z_1\text{.}\)

Conversely, if \(\phi_{\lambda}(z_1)=e^{-i\alpha}z_1\) for some \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T}\text{,}\) then \(p_2(\lambda)=0\) and \(\lambda \in \mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha})\text{.}\)

Proof.

By the definition of the reflection operation, \(p_2(\lambda)=0\) implies \(\tilde{p}_2(\lambda)=0\text{.}\) Next, if also \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) then \((p_1(\lambda)-e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(\lambda))^2=0\) so that \(p_1( \lambda)=e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)\text{.}\) But then

\begin{equation*} \phi(z_1,\lambda)=\frac{z_1\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)+0}{p_1(\lambda)+0}=e^{-i\alpha}z_1. \end{equation*}

Conversely, suppose

\begin{equation*} e^{-i\alpha}z_1=\frac{z_1\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)+\tilde{p}_2(\lambda)}{p_1(\lambda)+p_2(\lambda)z_1} \end{equation*}

for some unimodular \(\lambda\text{.}\) Then, comparing coefficients, we get \(p_2(\lambda)=0\) and as a direct consequence that \(\tilde{p}_2(\lambda)=0\text{.}\) Finally, we read off that \(e^{-i\alpha}=\frac{\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)}{p_1(\lambda)}\) and hence \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\text{,}\) as claimed.

Having seen what happens on the set \(\Lambda^{\sharp}\text{,}\) we now examine the behavior of \(Q_{\alpha}\) on \(\Lambda^{\flat}\text{.}\)

Lemma 4.9.

Suppose \(\lambda \in \Lambda^{\flat}\text{.}\) Then

  1. \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) if and only if \(p_1(\lambda)+e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)=0\text{;}\)
  2. if \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) then \(Q_{\alpha}\) vanishes to at least order \(2\) at \(\lambda\text{.}\)
Proof.

We first prove (1). By completing the square, we can rewrite \(Q_{\alpha}\) as

\begin{equation*} Q_{\alpha}(z_2)=(p_1(z_2)+e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(z_2))^2+4e^{i\alpha}(p_2(z_2)\tilde{p}_2(z_2)-p_1(z_2)\tilde{p}_1(z_2)). \end{equation*}

Since \(\lambda \in \Lambda^{\flat}\text{,}\) Lemma \ref{lem:collapsefiberlem} implies that \(p(\tau_1,\lambda)=\tilde{p}(\tau_1,\lambda)=0\) for some \(\tau_1\in \mathbb{T}\text{.}\) Solving for \(\tau_1\text{,}\) we deduce that \(\frac{p_1(\lambda)}{p_2(\lambda)}=\frac{\tilde{p}_2(\lambda)}{\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)}\) and then \(p_1(\lambda)\tilde{p}_1(\lambda)-p_2(\lambda)\tilde{p}_2(\lambda)=0\text{.}\)

Hence, \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=(p_1(\lambda)+e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1(\lambda))^2\) leading to the desired condition.

We turn to (2). Clearly, any zero of \((p_1+e^{i\alpha}\tilde{p}_1)^2\) occurs with even multiplicity. Next, we note that \(\left|\frac{p_2(z_2)}{p_1(z_2) }\right|^2\leq 1\) for \(z_2\in \mathbb{D}\text{,}\) for otherwise \(p\) would have a zero in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) Now, we note that

\begin{equation*} p_2(e^{it_2})\tilde{p}_2(e^{it_2})-p_1(e^{it_2})\tilde{p}_1(e^{it_2})=e^{int_2}\left(|p_2(e^{it_2})|^2-|p_1(e^{it_2})|^2\right) \end{equation*}

and since the expression on the right is a non-positive trigonometric polynomial, the Fejér-Riesz theorem implies that it is equal to \(-|r(e^{it_2})|^2\) for some polynomial \(r\text{,}\) with a zero at \(\lambda\) by the proof of (1).

We shall see later, in Example 5.4, that it may well happen that \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)\neq 0\) for certain \(\lambda \in \Lambda^{\flat}\text{.}\)

Theorem 4.10.

Suppose \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) for some \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T}\setminus (\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp})\text{.}\) Then \(\psi^{1}(\lambda)\) and \(\psi^{2}(\lambda)\) coincide as an element of \(\mathbb{T}\text{,}\) and \(\phi_{\lambda}\) has a parabolic fixed point at \(\psi^1_{\alpha}(\lambda)\text{.}\)

Note that \(\psi^{j}_{\alpha}\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{,}\) will in general exhibit branch points at points where \(Q_{\alpha}\) vanishes, and so \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) does not in general admit a description in terms of analytic functions near parabolic fixed points of \(\Phi\) in \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) This is in contrast to the results in [9] which state that level sets of RIFs can be parametrized in such a way at every point of \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\)

Proof.

Because of our assumptions, for each \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T} \setminus (\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp})\text{,}\) the fiber map \(\phi_{\lambda}\) is a M\"obius transformation not of the form \(e^{i\beta}z_1\text{.}\) Then, by the discussion in Section 3, \(\phi_{\lambda}\) either has two hyperbolic fixed points on the unit circle, or a parabolic fixed point on \(\mathbb{T}\text{,}\) or else an elliptic fixed point in \(\mathbb{D}\) with another fixed point lying in \(\overline{\mathbb{D}^c}\text{.}\) But \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) with \(\lambda \notin \Lambda^{\sharp}\) implies that \(\psi^{1}_{\alpha}(\lambda)=\psi^{2}_{\alpha}(\lambda)\text{.}\) Since \(\lambda\notin \Lambda^{\flat}\text{,}\) we are in the parabolic case and then necessarily \(\psi^{1}_{\alpha}(\lambda) \in \mathbb{T}\text{.}\)

Example 4.11.

Consider the polynomials \(p\) and \(\tilde{p}\) in appearing in the RIF \(\phi\) in Example 2.5 in Section 2. There, \(p_1(z_2)=3-z_2\) and \(p_2(z_2)=-1\text{,}\) while \(\tilde{p}_1(z_2)=3z_2-1\) and \(\tilde{p}_2(z_2)=-z_2\text{.}\) Then \(Q_{\alpha}(z_2)=16z_2^2-28z_2+16\text{,}\) and this polynomial has conjugate roots on \(\lambda_{1,2}=\frac{1}{8}(7\pm \sqrt{15}i)\in\mathbb{T}\text{.}\) The corresponding fibers \(F_{\lambda_{1,2}}\) bound the rotation belt of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

Example 4.12.

It may well happen that \(Q_{\alpha}\equiv 0\text{,}\) so that most fixed points are parabolic. This can be seen in Example 2.3 from Section 2. In that case, we have \(p_1(z_2)=3-z_2\) and \(p_2(z_2)=-(1+z_2)\) as well as \(\tilde{p}_1(z_2)=3z_2-1\) and \(\tilde{p}_2(z_2)=-(1+z_2)\text{,}\) and if we insist on the normalized choice \(\alpha=\pi\text{,}\) then indeed \(Q_{\alpha}\) is the zero polynomial. As we saw before, the set \(\{(1,e^{it_2})\colon t_2\neq 0,\pi\}\) consists of parabolic fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

This example also illustrates the need to assume \(\lambda \notin \Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp}\text{.}\) Namely, \(\phi(z_1,1)=1\) for \(\lambda=1\in \Lambda^{\flat}\) and \(\phi(z_1,-1)=-z_1\) for \(\lambda=-1 \in \Lambda^{\sharp}\text{,}\) and neither case leads to a parabolic fixed point.

Theorem 4.13.

Suppose \(\Phi\) is a degree \((1,n)\) RISP with associated \(Q_{\alpha}\) not identically zero. Then the number of rotation belts for \(\Phi\) is less than or equal to

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}\#\left[(\mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha}) \setminus \Lambda^{\flat})\cap \mathbb{T}\right]. \end{equation*}
Proof.

Note that \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) is parametrized by the analytic functions \(\psi^{1,2}_{\alpha}\) away from the branch points, and that the fiber map derivative \(\frac{d}{dz_1}\phi_{\lambda}\) is continuous off \(\iota(\Lambda^{\flat})\text{,}\) see Lemma 4.14 below. Thus, within a rotation belt \(B(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\) the composite map \(\lambda \mapsto \mathfrak{p}(\lambda) \in \mathbb{T}\) is continuous. Now if a curve component of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) containing elliptic fixed points were to meet \(\mathbb{T}^2\) in a hyperbolic fixed point having \(|\mathfrak{p}(\lambda)|\neq 1\text{,}\) this would force a discontinuity in the multiplier at \(\lambda_1\) or \(\lambda_2\text{.}\) Hence, the boundary fiber of a rotation belt contains a parabolic point, or is contained in \(\iota(\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp})\text{.}\)

Next, we observe that neither of the boundary fibers \(F_{\lambda_1}, F_{\lambda_2}\) can be collapsing. For if, say, \(F_{\lambda_1}\) was collapsing, then it could contain at most finitely many singularites \((\tau_1,\lambda_1), \ldots, (\tau_m, \lambda)\) of \(\phi\) by Lemma 3.3. But the collapsing of \(F_{\lambda_1}\) would imply that \(\Phi\) had discontinuities as \(\lambda \to \lambda_1\) in \(B(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)\) along vertical lines \(\{\tau\}\times \mathbb{T}\subset \mathbb{T}^2\) for \(\tau\neq \tau_1, \ldots, \tau_m\text{,}\) which is impossible. Hence, by Theorem Theorem 4.10, this implies that the fibers \(F_ {\lambda_1}\) and \(F_{\lambda_2}\) associated with a rotation belt \(B(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)\) contain a parabolic fixed point, or, if \(\lambda \in \mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha})\cap \Lambda^{\sharp}\) and \(\alpha=0\text{,}\) have fiber map \(\phi_{\lambda}=z_1\text{.}\)

Note that \(\deg Q_{\alpha} \leq 2n\text{,}\) so that \(Q_{\alpha}\) has at most \(2n\) zeros on \(\mathbb{T}\) counting multiplicities. It may well happen, however, that \(\Phi\) has fewer than \(n\) rotation belts if \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda_j)=0\) for some \(\lambda_j\in \Lambda^{\flat}\text{;}\) see Section 5 for an example with \(n=2\) and a single rotation belt. Similarly, examples show that the situation where a rotation belt is bounded by a fiber where the fiber map is equal to the identity can indeed arise.

Lemma 4.14.

Let \(\mathfrak{p}(\lambda)\) denote the multiplier of \(\phi_{\lambda}\) at a point \(\lambda \in \overline{\mathbb{D}}\text{.}\) Then \(\lambda\mapsto \mathfrak{p}(\lambda)\) is an analytic function on each component of

\begin{equation*} \Gamma(\phi)\setminus [\iota(\mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha}))\cap\iota(\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp})]. \end{equation*}
Proof.

Note that for each \(\lambda \in \mathbb{T}\setminus (\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp})\text{,}\) the Möbius map \(\frac{d}{dz_1} \phi_ {\lambda}\) is non-trivial and has no critical points. This implies that \(\lambda \mapsto \frac{d}{dz_1} \phi_ {\lambda}(\psi^{j}_{\alpha}(\lambda))\) is analytic off \(\mathcal{Z}(Q_{\alpha})\) and \(\Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp}\text{.}\)

Lemma 4.14 has the following consequence for rotations belts.

Lemma 4.15.

Suppose \(B(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\) is a rotation belt for \(\Phi=(\phi,z_2)\) and that \(\mathfrak{p}(\lambda)\) is non-constant on \((\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\text{.}\) Then uncountably many \(\phi_{\lambda}\) are conjugate to an irrational rotation.

Proof.

By assumption \(-i\log(\mathfrak{p}(\lambda))\) is a non-constant smooth function taking values in \([-\pi, \pi]\text{,}\) and since moreover \(-i\log(\mathfrak{p}(\lambda_{1,2}))=0\text{,}\) the statement follows from the intermediate value theorem.

Subsection 4.3 Hyperbolic fixed points and SF-points for simple RISPs

We now examine the dynamical behavior of \(\Phi\) in the vicinity of an SF-point.

All singularities of a RISP are points of \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{,}\) and lie in collapsing fibers by Lemma 3.3.

Lemma 4.16.

Let \((\tau_1, \lambda_1), \ldots, (\tau_n, \lambda_m)\) be SF-points of \(\Phi\text{.}\) Then \((\tau_j, \lambda_j) \in \Gamma(\Phi)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) for \(j=1,\ldots, m\text{.}\)

The proof of Theorem 4.13 shows that a collapsing fiber cannot be contained in a rotation belt \(B(\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\text{,}\) nor can it coincide with one of the boundary fibers \(F_{\lambda_1}\) or \(F_{\lambda_2}\text{.}\) We have already seen that SF-points can be accessed in \(\mathbb{T}^2\) via a component of \(\Gamma(\Phi) \cap \mathbb{T}^2\) in different ways. An SF-point may belong to a component of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\) consisting of parabolic points (Example \ref{ex:para}), or to one or more components of \(\Gamma(\Phi) \cap \mathbb{T}^2\) made up of hyperbolic fixed points (Example \ref{ex:fave}).

Theorem 4.17.

Let \((\tau, \lambda) \in \mathbb{T}^2\) be an SF-point of a RISP \(\Phi\) with associated polynomial \(Q_{\alpha}\) not identically zero.

  1. If \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)\neq 0\) then \((\tau, \lambda)\) belongs to a single component of \(\Gamma(\Phi) \cap \mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) Moreover, this component can be parametrized by an analytic function in some neighborhood of \((\tau, \lambda)\text{.}\)
  2. If \(Q_{\alpha}(\lambda)=0\) then \((\tau, \lambda)\) belongs to two components of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\cap \mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) If \(Q_{\alpha}\) vanishes to even order, then each of these components can be parametrized by an analytic function in some neighborhood of \((\tau, \lambda)\text{.}\)
Proof.

Item (1) follows from the definition of \(\psi^{j}\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{,}\) in (4.3) and (4.4) together with the observation that points in \(\Lambda^{\flat}\) cannot be elements of \(\Lambda^{\sharp}\text{.}\)

Let us turn to item (2). Lemma 4.9 asserts that \(Q_{\alpha}\) vanishes to order at least \(2\text{.}\) If \(Q_{\alpha}\) in fact vanishes to even order at \(z_2=\lambda\text{,}\) then the function \(\sqrt{Q_{\alpha}(z_2)}\) is analytic in a neighborhood of \(\lambda\text{.}\) Hence \(\psi^{j}\text{,}\) \(j=1,2\text{,}\) are analytic also.

In other words, a pair of components of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) coming together at an SF-point generically cross transversally. In fact, examination of a wide range of examples (see [28]) suggests that the compoments of \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) can always be parametrized by analytic functions in a neighborhood of an SF-point, but we have not been able to find an elementary proof.

As an example, the RISP in Example 5.4 has two SF-points, with two hyperbolic fixed point curves meeting at one of them, and a single hyperbolic fixed point curve passing through the other.
