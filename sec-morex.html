Section 5 Further examples

We conclude by examining two examples with more intricate singularities and dynamical behavior. Further examples and images can be found on the webpage [28].

Example 5.1.

Consider the rational inner function

\begin{equation} \phi(z_1,z_2)=-\frac{\tilde{p}(z)}{p(z)}=-\frac{4z_1z_2^2 - z_2^2 - 3z_1z_2 - z_2 + z_1}{4 - z_1 - 3z_2 - z_1z_2 + z_2^2},\label{AMYRIF}\tag{5.1} \end{equation}

which features as an example in the paper [3], and has been further studied in [9][Section 1.3] and [6][Example 5.2] (without the minus sign in front). Since \(\phi\) has a single singularity at \((1,1)\) and \(\phi^*(1,1)=1\text{,}\) the corresponding RISP \(\Phi\) has a SF-point at \((1,1)\text{.}\) We further check that \(\phi(1,z_2)=1\text{,}\) confirming the presence of a collapsing fiber.

In this example, \(\alpha=\pi\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} p(z)=p_1(z_2)+z_1p_2(z_2) \end{equation*}

with

\begin{equation*} p_1(z_2)=4-3z_2+z_2^2\quad \textrm{and}\quad p_2(z_2)=-(1+z_2). \end{equation*}

We have \(p_2(-1)=0\text{,}\) and as expected \(\phi(z_1,-1)=-z_1\text{,}\) a rotation of order \(2\text{.}\) A short computation shows that the associated polynomial \(Q_{\alpha}\) is given by

\begin{equation} Q_{\alpha}(z_2)=25(z_2-1)^2\left(z_2^2-\frac{14}{25}z_2+1\right).\label{AMYQ}\tag{5.2} \end{equation}

Then \(Q_{\alpha}(z_2)\) has a double root at \(\lambda_1=1\text{,}\) and two roots at \(\lambda_{2,3}=\frac{1}{25}(7\pm 24i)\text{.}\)

Figure 5.2 displays the dynamics of \(\Phi\) on \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) We notice a single rotation belt bounded by \(F_{\lambda_2}\) and \(F_{\lambda_3}\text{,}\) one fewer than the maximum given the degree of \(Q_{\alpha}\text{.}\) Also visible are two curves containing hyperbolic fixed points with a normal crossing at \((1,1)\text{,}\) which is contained in the collapsing fiber.

The hyperbolic fixed points are visible on a pair of curves parametrized by

\begin{equation*} \psi^{1,2}_{-1}(z_2)=\frac{1}{2(1+z_2)}\left(5 - 6 z_2 +5 z_2^2 \pm (-1 + z_2) \sqrt{25 - 14 z_2 + 25 z_2^2}\right). \end{equation*}

These curves exhibit a normal crossing at \((1,1)\text{,}\) the singularity of the RIF, reflecting the double root of \(Q_{\alpha}\) at \((1,1)\text{.}\) The branch point nature of the parabolic fixed points at \(\lambda_2\) and \(\lambda_3\) can be observed in Figure 5.3 which displays the absolute value of the functions \(\psi^{1,2}(e^{it_2})\text{.}\)

Example 5.4.

We turn to a RIF with multiple singularities on \(\mathbb{T}^2\) and multiple rotation belts. Consider the RIF \(\phi=-\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\) with

\begin{equation} p(z)=4 - z_1 + z_1z_2 - 3z_1z_2^2 - z_1z_2^3 \quad \textrm{and}\quad \tilde{p}(z)=4z_1z_2^3 - z_2^3 +z_2^2 - 3z_2 - 1,\label{multp}\tag{5.3} \end{equation}

a slightly modified form of [9][Example 7.4] (see also [6][Example 5.4]).

We have \(p(1,1)=0\) and \(p(-1,-1)=0\text{,}\) and a computation shows that \(\phi(z_1,1)=1\) and \(\phi(z_1,-1)=-1\text{,}\) confirming the presence of two collapsing fibers as guaranteed by Lemma 3.3, and so \(\phi\) has two SF-points on the \(2\)-torus.

We read off that

\begin{equation*} p_1(z_2)=4 \quad \textrm{and}\quad p_2(z_2)=z_2^3+3z_2^2-z_2+1 \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \tilde{p}_1(z_2)=4z_2^3 \quad \textrm{and} \quad \tilde{p}_2(z_2)=z_2^3-z_2^2+3z_2+1, \end{equation*}

and after some simplifications (using that \(\alpha=0\) here), we find that

\begin{equation} Q_{\alpha}(z_2)=4(z_2+1)^2(z_2^2+1)(5z_2^2-8z_2+5).\label{multQ}\tag{5.4} \end{equation}

The polynomial \(Q_{\alpha}\) has a double root at \(z_2=-1\) (the \(z_2\)-coordinate of the SF-point at \((-1,-1)\)), and simple roots at \(z_2=\pm i\) and \(z_2=\frac{1}{5}(4\pm3i)\text{.}\) On the other hand \(Q_{\alpha}(1)\neq 0\text{.}\) This is reflected in the images in Figure 5.5: \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) has two components coming together at \((-1,-1)\in \Lambda^{\flat}\) with a normal crossing, while \((1,1)\in \Lambda^{\flat}\) is a generic point, in the sense that \(\Gamma(\Phi)\) does not have a self-crossing. The simple zeros of \(Q_{\alpha}\) correspond to parabolic points bounding two distinct rotation belts, and these in turn lie between two arrangements of curves with hyperbolic fixed points.
