Section 1 Introduction and overview

Iteration of a rational function \(R(z)=\frac{q(z)}{p(z)}\) on the Riemann sphere, that is, the study

\begin{equation*} z\mapsto R^n(z)=(R\circ R \circ \cdots \circ R)(z) \quad (n=1,2,\ldots) \end{equation*}

on \(\mathbb{C}_{\infty}=\mathbb{C}\cup\{\infty\}\text{,}\) is a well-known topic in mathematics, discussed in many textbooks (e.g. [5, 11, 19]) and illustrated in beautiful computer images. The theory is quite mature, but important new results are still being discovered. The higher-dimensional theory, addressing iteration of polynomial or rational mappings \(R\colon \mathbb{C}^n\to \mathbb{C}^n\) is of later date and perhaps less complete. See, for instance, [13, 25, 19], and the references therein, for basic overviews of dynamics in several complex variables.

In a different direction, considering self-maps of special bounded domains in \(\mathbb{C}^n\) (the unit disk in the complex plane, the unit \(n\)-ball) allows for the study of iteration of functions that are not necessarily defined throughout \(\mathbb{C}^n\text{,}\) and leads to interesting boundary phenomena not observed in the unbounded setting. An important example in this latter direction is the classical Denjoy-Wolff theorem [11] concerning fixed points of analytic self-maps of the unit disk; there are many subsequent extensions of the original result to different settings.

Our investigation concerns the study of the dynamics certain self-maps of the unit bidisk

\begin{equation*} \mathbb{D}^2=\{(z_1,z_2)\in \mathbb{C}^2\colon |z_j|\lt 1, j=1,2\} \end{equation*}

and seeks to establish some basic facts about their iteration theory. There are many works addressing iteration of analytic self-maps of the bidisk and higher-dimensional polydisks (see, for instance, the papers [1, 4, 12, 14, 15] and the thesis [20] for some background). Our focus is on obtaining detailed results for a restricted class of rational mappings by using elementary means.

We say that a mapping of the form

\begin{equation*} \Phi\colon \mathbb{D}^2\to \mathbb{D}^2 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (z_1,z_2)\mapsto (\phi_1(z_1,z_2), \phi_2(z_1,z_2)) \end{equation*}

is a rational inner mapping (RIM) if each component \(\phi_j\) is a rational inner function on \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) A rational inner function in turn is an analytic function of the form

\begin{equation*} \psi(z_1,z_2)=\frac{q(z_1,z_2)}{p(z_1,z_2)}, \end{equation*}

with \(q,p\in \mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) and \(p(z)\neq 0\) in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{,}\) which is bounded in \(\mathbb{D}^2\) and has unimodular non-tangential boundary values at almost every point \(\zeta \in \mathbb{T}^2=\{(\zeta_1, \zeta_2)\in \mathbb{C}^2\colon |\zeta_j|=1, j=1,2\}\text{.}\) We recall the basic fact that \(\mathbb{T}^2\) is the distinguished boundary of the bidisk, the subset of the boundary \(\partial \mathbb{D}^2\) where most interesting function-theoretic phenomena on \(\mathbb{D}^2\) are observed and the maximum modulus principle is supported. By a theorem of Knese [18], if \(\phi_j\) is rational inner then the boundary values \(\phi_j^*(\zeta)\) exist as unimodular numbers at every point of \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{,}\) and so we can view a rational inner mapping \(\Phi\) as inducing a map \(\Phi\colon \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\to \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\text{,}\) with \(\Phi\) sending \(\mathbb{T}^2\) to \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) In the same way, the \(n\)th iterate of \(\Phi\)

\begin{equation*} \Phi^n(z_1,z_2)=(\Phi\circ \Phi\circ\cdots \circ\Phi)(z_1,z_2) \end{equation*}

can be viewed as a mapping of the closure of the bidisk into itself that fixes the distinguished boundary.

Iteration of certain classes of rational inner functions on the bidisk and on \(\mathbb{T}^2\) has been considered in several papers, for instance in [23, 22]; see also [26] for some applications. In those works, the component maps \(\phi_j\) are assumed to be of the special type

\begin{equation*} \psi_j(z_1,z_2)=B_{j,1}(z_1)\cdot B_{j,2}(z_2) \end{equation*}

where \(B_{j,k}\) are one-variable finite Blaschke products. Recall that these are functions \(B\colon \mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}\) of the form

\begin{equation*} B(z)=e^{i\alpha}z^m\prod_{k=1}^n\frac{z-a_k}{1-\overline{a}_kz}, \end{equation*}

where \(\{a_1,\ldots, a_n\}\subset \mathbb{D}\text{,}\) \(\alpha\in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and \(m,n\in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) It is apparent that a finite Blaschke product extends continuously to the unit circle \(\mathbb{T}\text{,}\) but the dynamics of one-variable Blaschke products nevertheless exhibit complicated features; see for instance [19] Chapter 15. Similarly, the works [23, 22] uncover a rich dynamical structure associated with two-dimensional Blaschke products.

In contrast with one-variable Blaschke products, a general rational inner function \(\psi=q/p\) in \(\mathbb{D}^2\) can have boundary singularities: these occur at points \(\tau=(\tau_1,\tau_2)\in \mathbb{T}^2\) where \(q(\tau_1,\tau_2)=p(\tau_1,\tau_2)=0\text{,}\) and represent a genuinely new higher-dimensional phenomenon. The function \(\psi\) is in general discontinuous at \(\tau \in \mathbb{T}^2\text{,}\) even though \(\psi^{*}(\tau)\) always exists, meaning that a RISP \(\Phi\) need not be a continuous self-map of \(\overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\text{.}\) This fact will be the source of several interesting phenomena that we observe in this paper.

Apart from some preliminary observations concerning RIMs, we mostly restrict our attention to rational inner skew-products (RISPs). These are rational inner mappings of the special form

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z_1,z_2)=(\phi_1(z_1,z_2), \phi_2(z_2)), \end{equation*}

with \(\phi_1\) rational inner in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{,}\) and \(\phi_2\) a rational inner function in one variable, viewed as a function on \(\mathbb{D}^2\) in the obvious way. Skew-products have been studied extensively in the polynomial setting, see e.g. [16, 21] and the references therein. In fact, we focus on the very simplest case of skew-mappings

\begin{equation} (z_1,z_2)\mapsto (\phi(z_1,z_2), z_2),\label{eq-RISPdef}\tag{1.1} \end{equation}

where \(\phi\) is a rational inner function in \(\mathbb{D}^2\) having bidegree \((1,n)\text{,}\) that is, degree \(1\) in \(z_1\) and degree \(n\in \mathbb{N}\) in \(z_2\text{.}\) (We drop the subscript in the first component to lighten notation.) Skew-products of the form (1.1) fix horizontal lines so that the main dynamics take place along one-dimensional sets, but even this apparently very simple case gives rise to interesting behavior, as is suggested in the images below. The advantage in working with such low-degree RISPs is that the extremely simple classification of fiber dynamics (discussed below) allows us to focus in detail on the global features of iteration on \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\)

We proceed to describe the contents of our paper. We begin by examining three elementary examples of RISPs in Section [provisional cross-reference: sec-Ex]. These examples serve as guides for our general investigations and illustrate most of the features we uncover in a more general setting. In Section [provisional cross-reference: sec-Prelim] we introduce basic definitions for RIMs and RISPs, record some facts about RIFs and their numerator and denominator polynomials, and discuss iteration of MÃ¶bius transformations in the unit disk. Section [provisional cross-reference: se-:main] contains the main results of our paper. After some preliminary remarks about general RIMs and certain associated essentially \(\mathbb{T}^2\)-symmetric polynomials, we focus on degree \((1,n)\) RISPs. We then introduce and study rotation belts associated with a RISP, strips on which the dynamical actions on fibers are conjugate to rotations, and give an estimate on their number. We investigate how different components of the fixed point set of a RISPs can come together at a singular fixed point, and give criteria for different configurations to be present. The main tool we use is a one-variable polynomial \(Q_{\alpha}\) built from the numerator and denominator polynomials of the first-component map \(\phi\text{.}\) We conclude in [provisional cross-reference: sec:morex] by exhibiting further examples of RISPs having more intricate dynamical behavior, and serving as illustrations of our main results.
