Example 2.8.

Our next example is

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z_1,z_2)=\left(-\frac{3z_1z_2-z_1-z_2-1}{3-z_1-z_2-z_1z_2},z_2\right). \end{equation*}

The first component map \(\phi=-\frac{\tilde{p}}{p}\) again has a unique singularity on \(\mathbb{T}^2\) at the point \((1,1)\text{,}\) with \(\phi^*(1,1)=1\text{,}\) and since \(\Phi(z_1,1)=(1,1)\text{,}\) the fiber \(\{z_2=1\}\) collapses. The fixed points of \(\Phi\) on the \(2\)-torus are determined by \(\tilde{p}-z_1p=0\text{,}\) and since \(\tilde{p}-z_1p=-(z_1-1)^2(z_2+1)\text{,}\) the fixed point consists of \(\{z_1=1\}\cup\{z_2=-1\}\text{,}\) a union of two lines.

Since \(\phi(z_1,-1)=z_1\text{,}\) we obtain \(\Phi(z_1,-1)=(z_1,-1)\text{,}\) confirming that all the points on \(\{z_2=-1\}\) are fixed. By contrast, all other fibers contain a single fixed point at \((1,z_2)\text{.}\) The dynamics of \(\Phi\) are shown in Figure 2: we see that when \(t_2>0\text{,}\) points are attracted to the \(t_2\)-axis from the right, and when \(t_2\lt 0\text{,}\) the fibers are attracted to the axis from the left. A concrete formula for \(\lim_{n\to \infty}\Phi\) is not readily apparent, but a computation shows that

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z_1}(z_1,z_2)=4\frac{(z_2-1)^2}{(3-z_1-z_2-z_1z_2)^2}, \end{equation*}

hence \(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z_1}(1,z_2)=1\text{.}\) As we will see later, this confirms that the fixed points on \(\{(1,\zeta_2)\}\) are neither attractive nor repelling in this example: they are so-called parabolic fixed points.
