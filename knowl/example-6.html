Example 4.12.

It may well happen that \(Q_{\alpha}\equiv 0\text{,}\) so that most fixed points are parabolic. This can be seen in Example 2.3 from Section 2. In that case, we have \(p_1(z_2)=3-z_2\) and \(p_2(z_2)=-(1+z_2)\) as well as \(\tilde{p}_1(z_2)=3z_2-1\) and \(\tilde{p}_2(z_2)=-(1+z_2)\text{,}\) and if we insist on the normalized choice \(\alpha=\pi\text{,}\) then indeed \(Q_{\alpha}\) is the zero polynomial. As we saw before, the set \(\{(1,e^{it_2})\colon t_2\neq 0,\pi\}\) consists of parabolic fixed points of \(\Phi\text{.}\)

This example also illustrates the need to assume \(\lambda \notin \Lambda^{\flat}\cup \Lambda^{\sharp}\text{.}\) Namely, \(\phi(z_1,1)=1\) for \(\lambda=1\in \Lambda^{\flat}\) and \(\phi(z_1,-1)=-z_1\) for \(\lambda=-1 \in \Lambda^{\sharp}\text{,}\) and neither case leads to a parabolic fixed point.
