We say that a mapping of the form

\begin{equation*} \Phi\colon \mathbb{D}^2\to \mathbb{D}^2 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} (z_1,z_2)\mapsto (\phi_1(z_1,z_2), \phi_2(z_1,z_2)) \end{equation*}

is a rational inner mapping (RIM) if each component \(\phi_j\) is a rational inner function on \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{.}\) A rational inner function in turn is an analytic function of the form

\begin{equation*} \psi(z_1,z_2)=\frac{q(z_1,z_2)}{p(z_1,z_2)}, \end{equation*}

with \(q,p\in \mathbb{C}[z_1,z_2]\) and \(p(z)\neq 0\) in \(\mathbb{D}^2\text{,}\) which is bounded in \(\mathbb{D}^2\) and has unimodular non-tangential boundary values at almost every point \(\zeta \in \mathbb{T}^2=\{(\zeta_1, \zeta_2)\in \mathbb{C}^2\colon |\zeta_j|=1, j=1,2\}\text{.}\) We recall the basic fact that \(\mathbb{T}^2\) is the distinguished boundary of the bidisk, the subset of the boundary \(\partial \mathbb{D}^2\) where most interesting function-theoretic phenomena on \(\mathbb{D}^2\) are observed and the maximum modulus principle is supported. By a theorem of Knese [18], if \(\phi_j\) is rational inner then the boundary values \(\phi_j^*(\zeta)\) exist as unimodular numbers at every point of \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{,}\) and so we can view a rational inner mapping \(\Phi\) as inducing a map \(\Phi\colon \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\to \overline{\mathbb{D}^2}\text{,}\) with \(\Phi\) sending \(\mathbb{T}^2\) to \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) In the same way, the \(n\)th iterate of \(\Phi\)

\begin{equation*} \Phi^n(z_1,z_2)=(\Phi\circ \Phi\circ\cdots \circ\Phi)(z_1,z_2) \end{equation*}

can be viewed as a mapping of the closure of the bidisk into itself that fixes the distinguished boundary.
