The bidegree \((1,1)\) RISP

\begin{equation*} \Phi(z_1,z_2)=\left(\frac{3z_1z_2-z_1-z_2}{3-z_1-z_2},z_2\right) \end{equation*}

has \(\Phi(1,1)=(1,1)\) so that \((1,1)\) is again a fixed point, but in this example,

\begin{equation*} \phi_1(z_1) = \phi(z_1,1)=\frac{2z_1-1}{2-z_1}, \end{equation*}

which is non-constant, meaning that \(\{z_2=1\}\) does not collapse into a point under \(\Phi\text{.}\) In fact, the component \(\phi\) does not have a singularity at \((1,1)\) or, indeed, at any point of \(\mathbb{T}^2\text{.}\) Instead, the points \((1,1)\) and \((-1,1)\) are fixed by \(\Phi\text{,}\) and since \(\phi_1'(1)=3\) and \(\phi_1'(-1)=\frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) the fixed point \((1,1)\) is repelling while \((-1,1)\) is attracting.
